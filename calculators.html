Closed by cutting services

$141.6M
Whole departments eliminated, smallest first

    Police, fire, and corrections are protected by the amendment, so cuts can only come from the roughly $247M of discretionary service departments. Departments are eliminated whole, smallest budget first. Because that pool is smaller than the $283.2M shortfall, even eliminating all of them leaves a remainder that taxes and fees must cover.

    Closed by raising taxes & fees

    $141.6M
    Equivalent new revenue the City would need to find

      The City's main legal options are raising the property tax millage (its consolidated charter allows up to about 20 mills) and, with voter approval, the local sales surtax. Recovering the full loss through the millage would concentrate the increase on a smaller taxable base, since the amendment removes much of the homestead value from that base. Sales surtax and fees apply to renters and lower-income households as well as homeowners.

      How to read this. The cut side draws from the discretionary service departments that are not protected by the amendment, eliminating them whole, smallest budget first, using FY2025-2026 figures. The tax side converts the same dollars into rough, illustrative equivalents: an increase in the property tax millage (scaled from the City's current ratio of about 11.19 mills to roughly $1.19B in property tax revenue) and an equivalent local sales surtax (a one-cent Duval surtax raises on the order of $250-260M a year). These are scale estimates for public understanding, not precise rate forecasts. The sales and property tax bases are not interchangeable, and the still-taxable property base would itself shrink under the amendment, so a real-world millage increase would likely need to be larger than the illustrative figure shown here.

      An estimated 210 positions cut from service departments

        At a given setting, departments are eliminated whole, smallest first, so a service is either fully funded or fully cut, not trimmed. The list shows which services are eliminated at the current slider position.

        If the gap is closed by taxing

        About $363 more per household each year

          Florida exempts most groceries and medicine, so the cost lands on other purchases: dining out, repairs, electronics, furniture, and similar taxable goods and services. Renters pay this too, even though they get none of the homestead savings.

          On the household figure. The per-household estimate spreads the tax share of the gap across Duval County's roughly 390,000 households. It is an average; a family that spends more on taxable goods pays more, and one that spends less pays less. Per-item costs show the extra tax on a single purchase at the higher rate, not a yearly total.
