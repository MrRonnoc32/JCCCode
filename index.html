A working analysis

What is the impact on Jacksonville if property taxes are cut?

Florida voters will consider a constitutional amendment that would raise the homestead exemption on most non-school property taxes, phasing up to the first $250,000 of a home's assessed value. For Jacksonville, current estimates put the resulting revenue change at about $283 million a year. This page lays out where the money comes from and goes today, what the amendment would do, and the budget choices that would follow, so readers can weigh the tradeoffs for themselves.

Part One

How Jacksonville is taxed, in plain terms

Before the proposal makes sense, it helps to know the two main taxes a Jacksonville resident pays, how they are limited, and why the city cannot simply swap one for the other. Property tax and sales tax work in completely different ways under Florida's constitution.

What you pay in property tax today

Property tax is charged as a millage: one mill is $1 of tax for every $1,000 of taxable value. Your bill is the sum of several separate levies set by different bodies. For most of Jacksonville (the General Services district), the 2025 final rates were:

What you pay in sales tax today

Sales tax is charged on most retail purchases, not on what you own. In Duval County the rate is 7.5%, made of two parts:

6.0%

1.5%

Groceries and most medicine are exempt. Counties may add a local surtax of up to 2% in most cases; Duval currently levies 1.5%, so there is limited room left to raise it.

School versus non-school: why the difference matters

Florida's constitution treats school and non-school property taxes separately, and the proposed amendment only touches the non-school side. The $50,000 homestead exemption is split for exactly this reason: the first $25,000 applies to all levies, while the second $25,000 (covering value between $50,000 and $75,000) applies only to non-school levies. The proposed jump to $250,000 would exempt far more value from city and county taxes, but schools are handled under their own rules.

The constitutional limits already in place

10 + 10 mills

The constitution caps property tax at 10 mills each for a county, a municipality, and a school district, separately. Because Jacksonville's consolidated charter holds both the city and county taxing authority, it can legally levy up to about 20 mills for general government. At roughly 11.19 mills today, the city has unused legal room under that cap to raise the rate.

3% cap

The Save Our Homes amendment limits how fast a homesteaded property's taxable value can rise, to 3% a year or inflation, whichever is lower. Non-homestead property (rentals, businesses) is currently capped at a looser 10%; the proposed amendment would lower that cap to 5% a year beginning in 2027.

60% vote

Changing any of this requires a statewide constitutional amendment passed by 60% of voters, which is why the homestead proposal goes before voters at the next general election.

Taxes versus flat fees on your bill

Your annual bill includes more than one kind of charge. Some line items are ad valorem property taxes: they are calculated as a millage rate applied to your home's taxable value, and homestead exemptions reduce them. Other line items are flat fees, also called non-ad valorem assessments. These are billed alongside the property tax but charged as a set amount per household or per property rather than as a percentage of value, so homestead exemptions do not reduce them. Both are real charges on the same bill; the difference is how each one is calculated. Two of the largest flat fees are:

Solid waste fee
A flat $354 per year per home in 2026 (rising to $384 in 2027), charged to every residence regardless of value. By interlocal agreement with the Beaches and Baldwin, Jacksonville is barred from using property tax to pay for garbage collection, which is why it is a separate fee. A low-income exemption holds it at $151.80.
Stormwater fee
A separate flat assessment for drainage system upkeep, also billed as a non-ad valorem assessment and unaffected by homestead status.

Other consumption-based levies the city collects include the utility service tax (on electric, water, and gas bills) and the communication services tax (on phone and cable). Like sales tax, these are charged on usage rather than property, and they apply to renters and owners alike. They are capped by state law and tend to rise and fall with the economy.

How these revenue sources differ

Property tax is tied to assessed value, set locally within the millage cap, and relatively steady year to year; it funds the largest share of day-to-day services. Sales taxes, flat fees, and excise taxes are capped by state law, vary more with the economy, and are not tied to property value. Because the bases are not interchangeable, replacing a large amount of property tax revenue with these other sources involves tradeoffs. The rest of this page sets out what those tradeoffs would look like for Jacksonville.

Part Three

Has government spending really grown?

One argument in the property tax debate is that rising home values have produced surplus revenue and that spending has grown faster than the cost of services. Evaluating that claim requires adjusting the raw figures for two factors: inflation and population growth. This section presents both the unadjusted and adjusted numbers.

Nominal and inflation-adjusted figures differ

From 2015 to 2024, total governmental-activities spending rose 87.5% in raw (nominal) dollars. Over the same period the dollar lost value and Jacksonville added residents. Adjusting for inflation, real spending grew 42.2%. Adjusting further for the 16.6% population growth, real spending per resident grew about 22% over nine years, or roughly 2.2% a year.

Part Four

A $2 billion budget, and what is left to cut

The City's General Fund is about $2 billion. The proposed exemption would reduce General Fund revenue by roughly $283 million each year. The question that follows is whether the City could simply trim $283 million of spending. The answer is that it would be very challenging given our city's commitments. Because the shortfall is larger than that entire pool, closing it through cuts alone would mean eliminating whole categories of city services.

Part Five

What the amendment would do

The amendment raises the homestead exemption that applies to most non-school property taxes. Today the first $50,000 of a home's assessed value is exempt; the amendment phases that up to the first $250,000, reaching full effect in 2028. School district taxes are unaffected and keep their existing $25,000 exemption. If approved by Florida voters at the next general election, the amendment takes effect January 1, 2027.

TodayCurrent law
The first $50,000 of a home's assessed value is exempt from most city and county property taxes. About 14,000 Jacksonville homes pay nothing toward general services.
2027First step
Beginning January 1, the exemption rises to the first $150,000 of assessed value for existing homesteads.
2028Full effect
Beginning January 1, the exemption reaches the first $250,000 of assessed value. Starting in 2028, the exemption amounts adjust annually for inflation (CPI) when the change is positive.

Other provisions in the amendment

New-resident phase-in (5-year rule)

Anyone who establishes a Florida homestead on or after January 1, 2027, and who was not a permanent resident as of December 31, 2026, receives only a $50,000 exemption for their first five years, then moves to the full $250,000 exemption in the fifth year.

Lower assessment cap

For non-school levies, the annual cap on assessment increases for non-homestead residential property (9 units or fewer) and other real property drops from 10% to 5% beginning January 1, 2027, with the 10% cap remaining through 2026.

Restrictions on how property tax is spent

Local governments could spend property tax revenue only on defined core needs: public safety, education, infrastructure, natural resources, debt service and employee retirement obligations, and basic administration.

Portability of Save Our Homes

The amendment maintains and structures portability of the Save Our Homes benefit when a homeowner moves to a new homestead, capping the maximum transferable assessment difference at $500,000.

Special taxing districts

Local special taxing districts may raise their own homestead exemptions up to the remaining value of a property, but only if approved by local voters through a referendum.

Long-term direction

The amendment directs the Legislature to set a schedule, by general law, for the eventual full elimination of these property taxes on homesteads.

How the public-safety protection works

The amendment protects police and fire funding by law, but it does not replace the lost revenue. As a result, a revenue reduction would fall on the rest of the budget: services such as libraries, parks, road repair, code enforcement, and general operations. The calculator and budget tools later on this page let you see how a $283 million reduction would land across those non-public-safety services.

Largest dollar savings

Because the exemption is a fixed dollar amount, the maximum saving is capped at about $2,238 a year, the city tax on $200,000 of value. Any home assessed above the $250,000 threshold receives the same maximum dollar reduction, regardless of its total value.

Smaller dollar savings

Homes assessed under $100,000, about 40,000 Jacksonville parcels, would see a smaller reduction, around $267 a year, because their taxable value is already low.

No direct savings

Roughly 47% of Duval parcels, including rentals and businesses, receive no direct homestead savings. Renters do not receive the exemption, though any change in services would still affect them.

A note on the millage cap. Because Jacksonville's consolidated government holds both city and county taxing authority, it can legally levy up to about 20 mills, above today's ~11.19, so raising the rate is constitutionally possible. The amendment would change the picture in two ways: it would exempt more home value from tax, and separately restrict how the remaining property tax can be spent. Recovering $283 million through the property tax rate alone would mean a large rate increase concentrated on a smaller pool of still-taxable property. The calculator on the next page lets you explore that option alongside others.

          Methodology

          Sources and how the numbers were built

          The $283.2 million figure comes from a parcel-level analysis of the Florida Department of Revenue's 2025 Name-Address-Legal file for Duval County (file NAL26F202501), covering all 398,317 parcels. The method applies an added $200,000 reduction to each homestead's non-school taxable value, floored at zero, then multiplies by the City's general services rate of 11.191 mills. Homestead general-services revenue falls from $409.3 million to $126.1 million, a loss of $283.2 million.

          Budget and staffing figures are drawn from the City of Jacksonville General Fund Operating Fund (00111) summary for FY2025-2026, including the $2,017,752,830 proposed General Fund, $1,191,159,774 in property tax revenue, and $731,948,235 in salaries. Department budgets and employee caps come from the same adopted budget document.

          The proposed exemption change reflects Florida Senate Joint Resolution 2-F (2026) and companion House measures HJR 201 through 213. These raise the homestead exemption on non-school levies in stages (to $150,000 in 2027 and $250,000 in 2028, with inflation adjustments thereafter), phase in the benefit for new residents over five years, lower the annual assessment-increase cap on non-homestead property to 5% beginning in 2027, cap transferable Save Our Homes portability at $500,000, and restrict the use of remaining property tax revenue to defined core needs: public safety, education, infrastructure, natural resources, debt and retirement obligations, and basic administration. If approved by voters, the amendment takes effect January 1, 2027.

          The analysis estimates static revenue effects only. It does not model behavioral responses such as changes in housing prices or new construction, nor does it assume any state replacement funding. Tax-and-fee equivalents on the calculator are illustrative estimates of scale, not precise rate projections.

          Public-safety classification on this page follows the Sheriff's Office and Fire and Rescue organizational structure, including the Personnel and Professional Standards function within the Sheriff's Office. Where internal support functions are assigned affects the size of the non-public-safety base; summaries that count only the headline Sheriff's Office and Fire and Rescue totals arrive at a slightly larger non-public-safety figure.

          Regulatory questions: where is the room, and who decides?

          The calculator's tax-and-fee side raises obvious questions about what the City could actually do. These answers describe the rules as they stand; specifics can change with legislation, and none of this is legal advice.

          User fees like the solid waste and stormwater assessments are set by the City Council by ordinance, not by referendum, so they are among the few levers the City can pull on its own. That is exactly what happened with the solid waste fee: the Council voted to roughly double it (from $151.80 to $324, then $354, then $384) through the normal ordinance process. The limit is twofold. First, a fee must reasonably relate to the cost of the service it funds; it cannot be a general revenue grab dressed up as a fee, or it risks being challenged as a disguised tax. Second, fees are politically sensitive, the garbage fee passed only 12 to 7 after more than a decade of avoidance. So there is room on fees, but it is narrow and service-specific, not a way to replace hundreds of millions in general revenue.

          Very little. Duval's 1.5% surtax is not one tax but three separate half-cent (0.5%) levies stacked together, each authorized under a different part of Florida law (Section 212.055): a Charter County Transportation surtax, a Local Government Infrastructure surtax (set to expire at the end of 2030), and a third half-cent levy. For a county like Duval, state law effectively caps the combined general surtax around this 1.5% level, so the City cannot simply add another penny. Raising more would generally require waiting for an existing levy to expire and replacing it, or the Legislature creating new surtax authority. A 2024 law change (Chapter 2024-158) did remove old language that had excluded consolidated Duval from one surtax category, a reminder that this room is defined by Tallahassee, not Jacksonville.

          Most discretionary sales surtaxes can only be enacted or raised by a countywide voter referendum, and the ballot must describe the projects the money will fund. A few specific surtax types can be adopted by a vote of the county governing body instead, but the broad infrastructure and transportation levies Duval relies on are referendum-based. The proceeds stay in the county and are distributed between the county and its municipalities by interlocal agreement or a default state formula. In short: the City Council cannot raise the sales surtax on its own; voters decide.

          Counties can levy up to 12 cents per gallon of local option fuel taxes across three separate levies, and Jacksonville already collects most of this capacity (its budget shows constitutional, county, and multiple local-option fuel taxes). Two practical limits apply. Fuel tax revenue is legally restricted to transportation purposes, so it cannot backfill libraries, parks, or general services. And it is a declining base over time as vehicles grow more efficient, which makes it a poor long-term substitute for property tax. There is some unused headroom, but it is narrow and locked to roads.

          The realistic options are limited and each has constraints. The City can raise the property tax rate (its consolidated charter allows up to about 20 mills, above today's ~11.19), though this is the same tax the amendment reduces, and recovering the full amount would require a large increase on a smaller taxable base. It can raise service fees by ordinance, but only where a fee matches a service cost. It can ask voters for a higher or new sales surtax, but the base is near its statutory cap and the money is purpose-restricted. The City cannot levy a local income tax, which the Florida Constitution prohibits, one reason Florida local governments rely heavily on property and consumption taxes. Taken together, no single avenue would replace $283 million in general revenue on its own.
