A working analysis

What is the impact on Jacksonville if property taxes are cut?

Florida voters will consider a constitutional amendment that would raise the homestead exemption on most non-school property taxes, phasing up to the first $250,000 of a home's assessed value. For Jacksonville, current estimates put the resulting revenue change at about $283 million a year. Three facts frame what would follow.

1 · The core problem

The revenue cut is larger than what the City can cut

The proposed exemption would reduce Jacksonville's General Fund revenue by about $283 million a year. But once public safety is protected and debt and pension obligations are paid, only about $247 million of spending — roughly 12% of the budget — is realistically available to cut. Closing the gap with cuts alone is not mathematically possible.

2 · Where the money has gone

Most of the recent growth went to public safety

From 2015 to 2024, about 83% of the real, inflation-adjusted growth in city spending went to public safety — the Sheriff's Office, Fire and Rescue, and the Medical Examiner. Across everything else, inflation-adjusted spending per resident has been roughly flat or slightly down.

3 · The remaining option

The main lever left is the property tax rate

Because service cuts cannot cover the gap, and the local sales surtax and most fees are capped by state law, the main revenue lever the City fully controls is the property tax millage rate. Offsetting the full $283 million would mean raising it from about 11.19 mills to roughly 13.85 mills (the consolidated charter allows up to about 20). Put plainly: a measure intended to reduce property taxes would, to be offset locally, require raising the property tax rate.
