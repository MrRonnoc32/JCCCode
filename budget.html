Where the General Fund comes from, where it is spent, and how much the City could realistically cut once public safety, debt service, and pensions are accounted for.

The budget today

What Jacksonville's taxes and fees pay for

The City runs on a General Fund of about $2.02 billion for the 2025-2026 year. This section shows where that money comes from first, then where it is spent. About 55% of the General Fund goes to public safety. Here is the full picture before any change.

Where the money comes from

Property tax is the single largest source. The other pieces, such as state-shared money and utility taxes, are smaller and largely set outside the City's control.

Where the money is spent, department by department

Public safety is grouped into one line at the top; tap it to expand the individual police, fire, and corrections departments. Below it are the other services, each marked Other Services. Under the amendment, public-safety funding is protected from cuts, so any revenue loss would fall on these other services.

What's left to cut

A $2 billion budget, and what is left to cut

The City's General Fund is about $2 billion. The proposed exemption would reduce General Fund revenue by roughly $283 million each year. The question that follows is whether the City could simply trim $283 million of spending. The answer is that it would be very challenging given our city's commitments. And the cuts the City could make would dramatically impact core services.

Eliminating every cuttable service, all libraries, parks, road crews, code enforcement, planning, and the rest, still falls about $36 million short of the $283 million.

In short: public-safety funding is protected under the amendment, and debt and pension obligations have to be paid regardless. That leaves only about 12% of the budget as discretionary service spending. Because the shortfall is larger than that entire pool, closing it through cuts alone would mean eliminating whole categories of city services.
