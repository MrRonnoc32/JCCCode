1 The cut is larger than what the City can cut

Once public safety is protected and debt and pensions are paid, only about $247M of spending is realistically cuttable. The math does not close on cuts alone.

Eliminating every cuttable service still falls about $36M short.

2 Where recent spending has gone

From 2015 to 2024, about 83% of the real (inflation-adjusted) growth in city spending went to public safety. Total real spending per resident rose about 2.2% a year; non-public-safety spending per resident fell roughly 3% over the period.

3 The main remaining lever is the tax rate

Service cuts cannot cover the gap, and the sales surtax and most fees are capped by state law. Offsetting the full loss would mean raising the property tax millage from about 11.19 to ~13.85 mills (cap ~20). A measure to cut property taxes would, to be offset locally, require raising the property tax rate.

What the amendment would do

  • Raises the homestead exemption on most non-school taxes: first $150,000 in 2027, $250,000 in 2028 (school taxes keep the $25,000 exemption).
  • New residents get only a $50,000 exemption for five years, then the full amount.
  • Lowers the annual assessment-increase cap on non-homestead property from 10% to 5% (2027).
  • Restricts property-tax revenue to core needs: public safety, education, infrastructure, natural resources, debt/retirement, and basic administration.
  • Caps Save Our Homes portability at $500,000. Takes effect Jan 1, 2027 if approved by voters.

Who is affected

Higher-value homeowner: the largest dollar break, capped at about $2,238 a year.
Lower-value homeowner: a smaller break (homes under $100k save around $267).
Renter: no direct exemption, but exposed to any service cuts or tax/fee shifts.
Business / rental owner: no homestead benefit; still affected by millage and city services. Roughly 47% of Duval parcels get no direct homestead savings.
Sources & method. Revenue-loss estimate from a parcel-level analysis of the Florida Department of Revenue 2025 Name-Address-Legal file for Duval County (398,317 parcels), applying the added exemption to non-school taxable value at 11.191 mills. Budget and staffing from the City of Jacksonville FY2025-2026 General Fund. Spending trends from the City's ACFR statement of activities, 2015-2024, CPI- and population-adjusted. Tax-and-fee equivalents are illustrative estimates of scale, not precise forecasts; the analysis does not model behavioral responses or state replacement funding.
