The proposed change

What the amendment would do

The amendment raises the homestead exemption that applies to most non-school property taxes. Today the first $50,000 of a home's assessed value is exempt; the amendment phases that up to the first $250,000, reaching full effect in 2028. School district taxes are unaffected and keep their existing $25,000 exemption. If approved by Florida voters at the next general election, the amendment takes effect January 1, 2027.

TodayCurrent law
The first $50,000 of a home's assessed value is exempt from most city and county property taxes. About 14,000 Jacksonville homes pay nothing toward general services.
2027First step
Beginning January 1, the exemption rises to the first $150,000 of assessed value for existing homesteads.
2028Full effect
Beginning January 1, the exemption reaches the first $250,000 of assessed value. Starting in 2028, the exemption amounts adjust annually for inflation (CPI) when the change is positive.

Other provisions in the amendment

New-resident phase-in (5-year rule)

Anyone who establishes a Florida homestead on or after January 1, 2027, and who was not a permanent resident as of December 31, 2026, receives only a $50,000 exemption for their first five years, then moves to the full $250,000 exemption in the fifth year.

Lower assessment cap

For non-school levies, the annual cap on assessment increases for non-homestead residential property (9 units or fewer) and other real property drops from 10% to 5% beginning January 1, 2027, with the 10% cap remaining through 2026.

Restrictions on how property tax is spent

Local governments could spend property tax revenue only on defined core needs: public safety, education, infrastructure, natural resources, debt service and employee retirement obligations, and basic administration.

Portability of Save Our Homes

The amendment maintains and structures portability of the Save Our Homes benefit when a homeowner moves to a new homestead, capping the maximum transferable assessment difference at $500,000.

Special taxing districts

Local special taxing districts may raise their own homestead exemptions up to the remaining value of a property, but only if approved by local voters through a referendum.

Long-term direction

The amendment directs the Legislature to set a schedule, by general law, for the eventual full elimination of these property taxes on homesteads.

How the public-safety protection works

The amendment protects police and fire funding by law, but it does not replace the lost revenue. As a result, a revenue reduction would fall on the rest of the budget: services such as libraries, parks, road repair, code enforcement, and general operations. The calculator and budget tools later on this page let you see how a $283 million reduction would land across those non-public-safety services.

Largest dollar savings

Because the exemption is a fixed dollar amount, the maximum saving is capped at about $2,238 a year, the city tax on $200,000 of value. Any home assessed above the $250,000 threshold receives the same maximum dollar reduction, regardless of its total value.

Smaller dollar savings

Homes assessed under $100,000, about 40,000 Jacksonville parcels, would see a smaller reduction, around $267 a year, because their taxable value is already low.

No direct savings

Roughly 47% of Duval parcels, including rentals and businesses, receive no direct homestead savings. Renters do not receive the exemption, though any change in services would still affect them.

A note on the millage cap. Because Jacksonville's consolidated government holds both city and county taxing authority, it can legally levy up to about 20 mills, above today's ~11.19, so raising the rate is constitutionally possible. The amendment would change the picture in two ways: it would exempt more home value from tax, and separately restrict how the remaining property tax can be spent. Recovering $283 million through the property tax rate alone would mean a large rate increase concentrated on a smaller pool of still-taxable property.
