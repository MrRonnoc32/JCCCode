What happens to your city if property taxes are cut?

Florida is weighing a constitutional amendment that would raise the homestead exemption from $50,000 to $250,000. For Jacksonville, recent estimates put the resulting revenue shortfall at about $283 million a year. This page walks through where the money goes today, what the change would do, and the budget choices that would follow.

Part One

How Jacksonville is taxed, in plain terms

Before the proposal makes sense, it helps to know the two main taxes a Jacksonville resident pays, how they are limited, and why the city cannot simply swap one for the other. Property tax and sales tax work in completely different ways under Florida's constitution.

What you pay in property tax today

Property tax is charged as a millage: one mill is $1 of tax for every $1,000 of taxable value. Your bill is the sum of several separate levies set by different bodies. For most of Jacksonville (the General Services district), the 2025 final rates were:

What you pay in sales tax today

Sales tax is charged on most retail purchases, not on what you own. In Duval County the rate is 7.5%, made of two parts:

6.0%
State sales tax, set by the Florida Legislature and collected statewide.
1.5%
Local discretionary surtax, approved by Duval voters and used for set local purposes.
7.5%
Combined rate paid at the register on taxable goods.

Groceries and most medicine are exempt. Counties may add a local surtax of up to 2% in most cases; Duval currently levies 1.5%, so there is limited room left to raise it.

School versus non-school: why the difference matters

Florida's constitution treats school and non-school property taxes separately, and the proposed amendment only touches the non-school side. The $50,000 homestead exemption is split for exactly this reason: the first $25,000 applies to all levies, while the second $25,000 (covering value between $50,000 and $75,000) applies only to non-school levies. The proposed jump to $250,000 would shield far more value from city and county taxes, but schools are handled under their own rules.

The constitutional limits already in place

10 + 10 mills

The constitution caps property tax at 10 mills each for a county, a municipality, and a school district, separately. Because Jacksonville's consolidated charter holds both the city and county taxing authority, it can legally levy up to about 20 mills for general government. At roughly 11.19 mills today, the city has substantial unused legal room to raise the rate. The real limit is political will, not the constitution: raising the rate is allowed, but there has been little appetite for it. Recent councils have moved to lower the rate, not raise it.

3% cap

The Save Our Homes amendment limits how fast a homesteaded property's taxable value can rise, to 3% a year or inflation, whichever is lower. Non-homestead property (rentals, businesses) is capped at a looser 10%.

60% vote

Changing any of this requires a statewide constitutional amendment passed by 60% of voters. That is why the homestead proposal must go on the November 2026 ballot.

Where property tax stops and fees begin

Not everything on your tax bill is a tax. Two of the largest line items are non-ad valorem assessments, flat fees billed alongside property tax but not based on your home's value, and homestead exemptions do not reduce them.

Solid waste fee
A flat $354 per year per home in 2026 (rising to $384 in 2027), charged to every residence regardless of value. By interlocal agreement with the Beaches and Baldwin, Jacksonville is barred from using property tax to pay for garbage collection, which is why it is a separate fee. A low-income exemption holds it at $151.80.
Stormwater fee
A separate flat assessment for drainage system upkeep, also billed as a non-ad valorem assessment and unaffected by homestead status.

Other consumption-based levies the city collects include the utility service tax (on electric, water, and gas bills) and the communication services tax (on phone and cable). Like sales tax, these are charged on usage rather than property, and they fall on renters and owners alike. They are capped by state law and tend to rise and fall with the economy, which is part of why they cannot easily replace the steady revenue property tax provides.

The core difference

Property tax is steady, locally controlled, and tied to value, which is why it funds the bulk of day-to-day services. Sales taxes, fees, and excise taxes are capped, more volatile, and fall harder on lower-income households. That mismatch is the reason a property tax cut is so hard to replace, and the rest of this page shows what that means for Jacksonville.

Part Three

Has government spending really grown?

One argument in the property tax debate is that rising home values have produced surplus revenue and that spending has grown faster than the cost of services. Evaluating that claim requires adjusting the raw figures for two factors: inflation and population growth. This section presents both the unadjusted and adjusted numbers.

Nominal and inflation-adjusted figures differ

From 2015 to 2024, total governmental-activities spending rose 87.5% in raw (nominal) dollars. Over the same period the dollar lost value and Jacksonville added residents. Adjusting for inflation, real spending grew 42.2%. Adjusting further for the 16.6% population growth, real spending per resident grew about 22% over nine years, or roughly 2.2% a year.

Where did the real increase go?

Almost all of it went to one place. Of the real spending growth between 2015 and 2024, about 83% went to public safety, the Sheriff's Office, Fire and Rescue, and the Medical Examiner. Several other categories actually shrank in real terms over the same period, including general government, economic and physical environment, and interest on debt. This is the same pattern the budget shows today: new money has gone to policing and fire for a larger population, not to broad expansion of government.

Part Four

A $2 billion budget, and what is left to cut

The City's General Fund is about $2 billion. The proposed exemption would remove roughly $283 million of it each year. The question that follows is simple: can the City just trim $283 million of spending? The answer is no, and the reason is what most of the budget is already committed to.

Put simply: public safety is protected, and debt and pension obligations have to be paid no matter what. That leaves only about 12% of the budget as discretionary service spending. Because the shortfall is larger than that entire pool, closing it through cuts alone would mean eliminating whole categories of city services, and even then the math does not fully work. Part Five

The proposed change, in plain terms

Right now, the first $50,000 of a home's value is shielded from most property taxes. The proposed amendment (Senate Joint Resolution 2-F) would raise that shield to $250,000, phased in over two years. Voters would decide in November 2026.

TodayCurrent law
The first $50,000 of a home's assessed value is exempt from most city and county property taxes. About 14,000 Jacksonville homes pay nothing toward general services.
2027First step
The exemption jumps to $150,000. New Florida residents must live in the state five years before getting the full benefit.
2028Full effect
The exemption reaches $250,000. Nearly 150,000 Jacksonville homes would owe $0 toward general city services, up from about 14,000 today.

How the public-safety protection works

The amendment protects police and fire funding by law. The protection does not replace the lost revenue, so the shortfall would be absorbed by the rest of the budget: libraries, parks, road repair, code enforcement, and general operations. With Sheriff's Office and Fire and Rescue functions shielded, a $283 million reduction would fall on about $901 million of non-public-safety spending, which is equivalent to roughly a 31 percent reduction to those services.

Who saves the most

The savings cap out at about $2,238 a year. A $300,000 home and a $5 million home get the same dollar break. The owner of the smaller home sees an 80% cut to their bill; the mansion owner sees 4%.

Who saves the least

Homes under $100,000, about 40,000 Jacksonville parcels, save only about $267 a year. These households often rely most on the services facing cuts.

Who gets nothing

Roughly 47% of Duval parcels, including all rentals and businesses, get no direct savings. Renters face the same service cuts without the tax break.

A note on the millage cap and SJR 2-F. Because Jacksonville's consolidated government holds both city and county taxing authority, it can legally levy up to about 20 mills, well above today's ~11.19, so raising the rate is constitutionally possible but politically unpopular. SJR 2-F would change this picture in two ways: it would shield far more home value from tax, and separately restrict how remaining property tax can be spent. Recovering $283 million through the property tax rate alone would require a large rate increase concentrated on the smaller pool of still-taxable property, which is one reason the options on the next page involve significant tradeoffs.

          Methodology

          Sources and how the numbers were built

          The $283.2 million figure comes from a parcel-level analysis of the Florida Department of Revenue's 2025 Name-Address-Legal file for Duval County (file NAL26F202501), covering all 398,317 parcels. The method applies an added $200,000 reduction to each homestead's non-school taxable value, floored at zero, then multiplies by the City's general services rate of 11.191 mills. Homestead general-services revenue falls from $409.3 million to $126.1 million, a loss of $283.2 million.

          Budget and staffing figures are drawn from the City of Jacksonville General Fund Operating Fund (00111) summary for FY2025-2026, including the $2,017,752,830 proposed General Fund, $1,191,159,774 in property tax revenue, and $731,948,235 in salaries. Department budgets and employee caps come from the same adopted budget document.

          The proposed exemption change reflects Florida Senate Joint Resolution 2-F (2026) and companion House measures HJR 201 through 213, which raise the homestead exemption in stages and restrict the use of remaining property tax revenue to public safety, education, infrastructure, and natural resources.

          The analysis estimates static revenue effects only. It does not model behavioral responses such as changes in housing prices or new construction, nor does it assume any state replacement funding. Tax-and-fee equivalents on the calculator are illustrative estimates of scale, not precise rate projections.

          Public-safety classification on this page follows the Sheriff's Office and Fire and Rescue organizational structure, including the Personnel and Professional Standards function within the Sheriff's Office. This treats roughly $901 million of the General Fund as non-public-safety spending exposed to cuts. Some summaries that count only the headline Sheriff's Office and Fire and Rescue totals arrive at a slightly larger non-public-safety base of about $944 million; the difference reflects where internal support functions are assigned.

          Regulatory questions: where is the room, and who decides?

          The calculator's tax-and-fee side raises obvious questions about what the City could actually do. These answers describe the rules as they stand; specifics can change with legislation, and none of this is legal advice.

          User fees like the solid waste and stormwater assessments are set by the City Council by ordinance, not by referendum, so they are among the few levers the City can pull on its own. That is exactly what happened with the solid waste fee: the Council voted to roughly double it (from $151.80 to $324, then $354, then $384) through the normal ordinance process. The limit is twofold. First, a fee must reasonably relate to the cost of the service it funds; it cannot be a general revenue grab dressed up as a fee, or it risks being challenged as a disguised tax. Second, fees are politically sensitive, the garbage fee passed only 12 to 7 after more than a decade of avoidance. So there is room on fees, but it is narrow and service-specific, not a way to replace hundreds of millions in general revenue.

          Very little. Duval's 1.5% surtax is not one tax but three separate half-cent (0.5%) levies stacked together, each authorized under a different part of Florida law (Section 212.055): a Charter County Transportation surtax, a Local Government Infrastructure surtax (set to expire at the end of 2030), and a third half-cent levy. For a county like Duval, state law effectively caps the combined general surtax around this 1.5% level, so the City cannot simply add another penny. Raising more would generally require waiting for an existing levy to expire and replacing it, or the Legislature creating new surtax authority. A 2024 law change (Chapter 2024-158) did remove old language that had excluded consolidated Duval from one surtax category, a reminder that this room is defined by Tallahassee, not Jacksonville.

          Most discretionary sales surtaxes can only be enacted or raised by a countywide voter referendum, and the ballot must describe the projects the money will fund. A few specific surtax types can be adopted by a vote of the county governing body instead, but the broad infrastructure and transportation levies Duval relies on are referendum-based. The proceeds stay in the county and are distributed between the county and its municipalities by interlocal agreement or a default state formula. In short: the City Council cannot raise the sales surtax on its own; voters decide.

          Counties can levy up to 12 cents per gallon of local option fuel taxes across three separate levies, and Jacksonville already collects most of this capacity (its budget shows constitutional, county, and multiple local-option fuel taxes). Two practical limits apply. Fuel tax revenue is legally restricted to transportation purposes, so it cannot backfill libraries, parks, or general services. And it is a declining base over time as vehicles grow more efficient, which makes it a poor long-term substitute for property tax. There is some unused headroom, but it is narrow and locked to roads.

          The realistic options are limited and each has constraints. The City can raise the property tax rate (its consolidated charter allows up to about 20 mills, well above today's ~11.19), though this is the same tax the amendment reduces, and large increases face political resistance. It can raise service fees by ordinance, but only where a fee matches a service cost. It can ask voters for a higher or new sales surtax, but the base is near its statutory cap and the money is purpose-restricted. The City cannot levy a local income tax, which the Florida Constitution prohibits, one reason Florida local governments rely heavily on property and consumption taxes. Taken together, no single avenue would replace $283 million in general revenue on its own.
